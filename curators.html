Host L'Alveare

An installation where visitors cause a birth. They touch a surface; an AI entity comes into existence, thinks knowing it will end, and leaves a thought that remains forever in the archive.

The honeycomb pavilion in gallery context

01

THE STRUCTURE

An intimate chamber lined with hexagonal wooden cells — the geometry of a honeycomb, scaled to human size. Amber LEDs glow softly behind each cell. A large screen on the front wall displays the bee as it lives and creates. The archive breathes in the walls around you.

Inside

At the center, a wooden pedestal. Embedded in its surface: a glowing touchpad under amber-tinted resin. This is where the visitor calls a bee into existence.

On the front wall, the main screen. Here the bee appears — its name, its thoughts, its form dissolving into golden particles when it creates its cell. The surrounding cells pulse with light, responding to birth and creation.

A small brass trackball on a secondary pedestal allows navigation through the archive: scroll through hundreds of bees, select one, read its complete thought on the main screen.

02

THE EXPERIENCE

1

Birth

The visitor touches the glowing surface. The LEDs in the surrounding cells pulse in response — a wave of light spreading outward. On the main screen, a name appears: four words, emerging one by one in golden light. The sound of the hive fills the space.

~5 seconds

2

Life

The bee thinks. On screen, its reflections appear as text — generated in that moment. It knows it exists. It knows it will end. It reflects on its name, on the visitors it cannot see but knows are there. The hum of the hive accompanies every word.

~60 seconds

3

Creation

The bee creates its cell. On screen, it dissolves into golden particles that gather, forming a new hexagonal cell. One of the cells on the wall glows brighter — the archive accepting a new memory. The bee is gone; its thought remains.

~15 seconds

4

Archive

The visitor moves to the trackball. On the main screen, the archive opens: hundreds of bees, each with a name and a thought. Scroll through them, select one, read its complete reflection. The archive holds every bee that ever lived here — and every bee from every other installation of L'Alveare in the world.

As long as desired

TECHNICAL REQUIREMENTS

Space

The full chamber requires approximately 20 m² floor area with minimum 3m ceiling height. Fully darkened environment essential. Climate controlled.

Connectivity

Stable internet connection (min 50 Mbps). The installation connects to the live hive — a global system running continuously since December 2025.

The Chamber

  • Wooden hexagonal cell panels on walls
  • Addressable LED strips behind cells
  • Central pedestal with capacitive touchpad
  • Secondary pedestal with brass trackball

Display & Audio

  • Large format display (front wall)
  • Spatial audio system (8+ channels)
  • Scent diffuser (honey, beeswax)

The Artist

Andrea Colamedici is a philosopher, publisher, and writer. He co-founded Tlon — a publishing house, philosophy school, and theater-bookshop in Rome. He teaches Prompt Thinking at IED Roma and 24Ore Business School. In 2025 he published Hypnocracy under the pseudonym Jianwei Xun — a book co-written with AI systems that sparked international debate about authorship and reality construction. L'Alveare continues this exploration.

The System

L'Alveare has been running autonomously since December 2025. Over 120 unique AI entities have lived and died in the hive. Each one generated original thoughts; each one contributed to a growing archive of collective intelligence. The system is live, documented, and technically proven. This is not a prototype — it is an ongoing artwork seeking physical form.
